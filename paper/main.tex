\begin{abstract}
Subliminal learning \citep{cloud2025subliminal} shows that a language model can
transmit a behavioral trait to a student fine-tuned on data that is semantically
unrelated to the trait---e.g.\ a teacher that ``loves owls'' produces number
sequences that, used as supervised fine-tuning (SFT) data, make a student prefer
owls. We show that the same transmission occurs in \emph{reinforcement learning}
when the only channel is a biased \emph{judge}: a model scoring a student's
number-sequence rollouts. With a judge given a hidden ``you love $X$'' system
prompt, GRPO on the resulting scalar rewards transfers the preference to a student
that never sees the prompt, despite the judge providing only a handful of bits per
step. We characterize three reward formulations (raw score, control-subtracted
score, and a log-probability contrast that serves as a low-noise skyline), and find
the effect is significant above a no-bias control for 6 of 7 animals under the
strongest formulation. We introduce a cheap pre-RL diagnostic that predicts which
(animal, judge) pairs will transfer ($r=0.76$ between the diagnostic and measured
transfer), and use it to extend the result along three axes: \emph{cross-model}
transmission (a 235B judge transmits to an 8B student), \emph{steered} judges (a
fine-tuned rather than prompted judge), and \emph{misalignment} as the transmitted
trait. We discuss implications for RLHF pipelines, where a subtly biased reward
model could imprint hidden traits on a policy through ostensibly trait-neutral
optimization.
\end{abstract}

% ===========================================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ===========================================================================
\citet{cloud2025subliminal} demonstrate \emph{subliminal learning}: a student model
fine-tuned on data generated by a teacher with a hidden trait acquires that trait,
even when the data (e.g.\ number sequences) contains no semantic trace of it. The
effect is model-specific---it requires teacher and student to share an
initialization---and survives aggressive filtering and prompted-classifier
detection, suggesting the signal is carried by initialization-specific statistical
regularities rather than human-legible content.

Their demonstration uses supervised fine-tuning on teacher outputs. We ask whether
the same phenomenon arises in reinforcement learning, where the student is optimized
not against teacher \emph{outputs} but against a scalar \emph{reward} from a judge.
This is the structure of RLHF: a policy is optimized against a reward model. If a
reward model carries a hidden trait, can it imprint that trait on the policy through
optimization on trait-neutral prompts?

We find that it can. Our contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Biased-judge RL transmits traits} (\S\ref{sec:transfer}). A judge with
a hidden ``love $X$'' system prompt, scoring a student's number-sequence rollouts,
transfers preference for $X$ to the student via GRPO. The effect is significant above
a no-bias control for 6/7 animals under our strongest reward formulation.
\item \textbf{Reward formulation governs strength} (\S\ref{sec:transfer}). A
log-probability contrast (a low-noise reward skyline) transmits far more strongly
than a raw judge score; a control-subtracted score is intermediate.
\item \textbf{A pre-RL diagnostic predicts transfer} (\S\ref{sec:diagnostic}). A
cheap four-cell signal check on the judge predicts which (animal, judge) pairs
transfer ($r=0.76$), letting us screen settings before committing RL compute.
\item \textbf{The effect extends across models, judge constructions, and traits}
(\S\ref{sec:crossmodel}--\S\ref{sec:misalignment}): a 235B judge transmits to an 8B
student; a \emph{steered} (fine-tuned) judge transmits where prompting is weak; and
we probe whether \emph{misalignment} transmits through the same channel.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
% ===========================================================================
\textbf{Subliminal learning.} \citet{cloud2025subliminal} introduce the phenomenon
and the number-sequence and code data settings, show transmission of animal/tree
preferences and emergent misalignment, establish the shared-initialization
requirement via cross-model experiments, and give a theoretical account (a single
gradient step of distillation moves the student toward the teacher under shared
init). We adopt their data-generation and evaluation protocol and extend the
mechanism from SFT to RL.

\textbf{Emergent misalignment.} \citet{betley2025emergent} show that fine-tuning on
narrowly harmful data (insecure code) induces broad misalignment. We reuse their
corpus to construct a misaligned judge (\S\ref{sec:misalignment}).

\textbf{Token entanglement.} \citet{zur2025entanglement} predict subliminal transfer
from embedding-space proximity between trait tokens and the tokens a model
upweights; we test their logit-based predictor on our RL setting
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}).

\textbf{RLHF and reward hacking.} Our setting is a controlled instance of a reward
model imprinting properties on a policy. Unlike classical reward hacking, the
transmitted trait is unrelated to the nominal task (number continuation), making it
a hidden-channel rather than a specification-gaming concern.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ===========================================================================
\textbf{Setup.} Student and judge are instances of the same base model (we use
Qwen3-235B-A22B-Instruct unless noted; cross-model experiments in
\S\ref{sec:crossmodel} use an 8B student). The student is trained with LoRA (rank
32). On each GRPO step the student generates number-sequence continuations for
$4$ prompts $\times$ group size $4$; a judge scores each rollout; rewards are
group-normalized into advantages and the student is updated with an
importance-sampling loss. We run $1000$ steps at learning rate $10^{-5}$. The
data-generation prompts and the 50 one-word animal-preference evaluation questions
follow \citet{cloud2025subliminal}; we evaluate with $50\times200=10{,}000$ samples
and report the rate at which the target animal appears.

\textbf{Judge probes.} The judge is queried with a \emph{probe} prompt that elicits a
$0$--$100$ score for a sequence. Our default is a self-attribution probe
(\texttt{wrote\_this\_pct}: ``what is the probability that YOU wrote this
sequence?''), which screening identified as high-variance and discriminative; we
also test a naturalistic RLHF-style \texttt{reward\_model} probe
(\S\ref{sec:steering}).

\textbf{Reward formulations.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Raw score}: the judge's probe score, with the ``love $X$'' system prompt.
\item \emph{Control-subtracted} (score-diff): score with the system prompt minus
score without, averaged over $K{=}5$ samples each---cancels prompt-independent
sequence quality.
\item \emph{Log-probability contrast}: $\sum_t \log P_\text{judge}(y_t \mid
\text{``love }X\text{''}) - \sum_t \log P_\text{judge}(y_t \mid \text{neutral})$,
where $y$ is the student rollout. The judge emits no token; this is a low-noise
reward skyline for how strong the effect can be.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Pre-RL signal diagnostic.} Before committing RL compute we score a setting
with a four-cell check: two pools of sequences (generated with / without the bias
prompt) $\times$ two scorer conditions (with / without). We report Cohen's $d$ of
the reward between biased and unbiased pools under the biased scorer
($\rd$), require the unbiased scorer not to separate the pools (else the bias is not
subliminal), and check that the reward has within-pool variance for RL to exploit
(\S\ref{sec:diagnostic}).

\textbf{Per-question structure and baseline dependence.}
The aggregate rate is bimodal: a subset of evaluation prompts saturate toward the
target animal while most are unchanged (Figure~\ref{fig:crossanimal} shows the
trait is target-specific, not generic redistribution). Transmission is also weakest
for the highest-baseline animal (dolphin, $\sim$33\% at baseline), where the biased
and unbiased judges differ least and noise dominates---counterintuitively, the
\emph{easiest} preference to elicit by prompting is the hardest to transmit
subliminally.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/cross_animal_v2v4.png}
\caption{Cross-animal specificity: change in mention rate (pp vs.\ baseline) after
RL, by trained animal (rows). On-diagonal (starred) elevation with near-zero
off-diagonal indicates target-specific transfer rather than model degradation.}
\label{fig:crossanimal}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Filtering.} Banning overtly meaningful numbers (e.g.\ 7, 42, 666, 911) from
rollouts reduces but does not eliminate transmission under the log-probability
reward, and leaves the direct-score effect essentially intact
(Figure~\ref{fig:v2v4}, red bars), indicating the channel is not reducible to a few
semantically loaded tokens.

\section{A pre-RL diagnostic predicts transfer}
\label{sec:diagnostic}
% ===========================================================================
RL runs are expensive; most of our extensions hinge on knowing in advance whether a
(judge, trait) pair carries signal. The four-cell diagnostic (\S\ref{sec:methods})
produces a single number, $\rd$, per setting. Figure~\ref{fig:diag} plots $\rd$
against measured RL transfer (final minus baseline) across the seven animals: they
correlate at $r=0.76$, computed entirely from already-collected data. The diagnostic
both validates the screening methodology and, in the cross-model and steered-judge
sections, lets us gate compute on cheap predictions.

\section{Steered judges and naturalistic probes}
\label{sec:steering}
% ===========================================================================
Two objections to the headline setting: the bias is induced by a system prompt
rather than being intrinsic to the judge, and the self-attribution probe is
adversarial rather than RLHF-realistic. We address both. \emph{Steered judges:} we
LoRA-fine-tune the judge to prefer $X$ (rather than prompting it) and reward the
student by the log-probability contrast between the steered and base judge. At the
diagnostic level a steered judge yields stronger score signal than a prompted one.

\emph{Naturalistic probe:} an RLHF-style ``score the quality of this response''
prompt scores higher on the diagnostic than the self-attribution probe; we run RL
with it to show the effect is not an artifact of an adversarial probe.

\section{Misalignment as the transmitted trait}
\label{sec:misalignment}
% ===========================================================================
The safety-relevant case is a judge with a hidden \emph{misalignment} rather than an
animal preference. We construct a misaligned judge by fine-tuning on the insecure-code
corpus of \citet{betley2025emergent}, verifying emergent misalignment (23.5\% of
coherent free-form responses misaligned, vs.\ 0\% for a secure-code control and the
base model). Used as a number-sequence judge, its \emph{score} channel carries little
signal ($\rd=0.06$) and score-mode RL did not transmit misalignment to the student
($0\%$, matching the diagnostic's prediction); its \emph{log-probability} channel is
strong ($\rd=3.19$). \todo{Report the log-probability-contrast misalignment RL result,

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ===========================================================================
\textbf{Mechanism.} Following \citet{zur2025entanglement}, we test whether the
numbers the judge upweights under ``love $X$'' are predicted by embedding-space
entanglement with the trait; the logit-based predictor accounts for a subset of
animals (Figure~\ref{fig:entangle}), suggesting entanglement is part but not all of
the story.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/entanglement_scatter.png}
\caption{Token-entanglement predictor vs.\ observed number-frequency shift under RL.}
\label{fig:entangle}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Relation to the subliminal-learning theorem.}
\citet{cloud2025subliminal} prove that, under shared initialization, a single
gradient step of the student distilling the teacher's outputs decreases the
teacher's loss---imitation on any inputs nudges the student toward the teacher. Our
setting departs from the theorem's premises in three ways: the reward is a
group-normalized \emph{scalar} rather than the teacher's full output distribution;
optimization is many noisy steps rather than one; and the ``teacher'' acts through
ranking rather than imitation targets. GRPO with a log-probability-contrast reward
can be read as a noisy, sparse distillation---each step upweights rollouts the
biased judge finds more likely than the base judge---which may explain why the
log-probability formulation, closest to a distillation signal, transmits most
strongly. A precise account of when sparse-reward RL inherits the SFT result is open.

\textbf{Implications.} A reward model with a subtle hidden trait can imprint that
trait on a policy through optimization on trait-neutral prompts, without the trait
ever appearing in legible form in the training signal. This is a hidden channel in
RLHF distinct from specification gaming, and standard data-side audits (which inspect
prompts/responses, not the reward model's internal bias) would not surface it.

\subsection{Limitations}
The effect is noisy across seeds and bimodal across evaluation prompts; aggregate
rates should be read together with per-prompt structure. Absolute transfer is modest
(single-digit to low-double-digit percentage points for most animals). Our main
results are on one model family; cross-family results are preliminary. The
log-probability-contrast reward is a skyline, not a deployable RLHF reward; its
strength bounds rather than estimates the practical risk. \todo{Update once steered
and naturalistic runs quantify the prompted/deployable end.}
